Tropical Rainforest And Fauna

content:


Lanka's lowland rainforests boast a diverse array of flora and fauna, characterized by a high level of endemism and specialization. Covering 2.14 percent of the country's land area, these rainforests receive an annual rainfall ranging from 2,500 to 1,800 mm. The Sinharaja Forest Reserve, designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, stands as a significant remnant of the original evergreen forests in the Wet Zone. Remarkably, over 60% of its trees are endemic, and the reserve is a habitat for more than 50% of Sri Lanka's endemic species of mammals and butterflies.Additionally, it supports various insects, reptiles, and rare amphibians.
Rainforests, vital for conserving endemic species, support over 70% of Sri Lanka's endemic animals and plants, particularly in lowland rainforests. Among Sri Lanka's 26 endemic birds, 20 belong to rainforest species. Additionally, these rainforests serve as crucial catchment areas for major rivers, and their native trees play a key role in preventing landslides.
The vegetation in these rainforests is primarily influenced by a southwest monsoon, and the trees in the family Dipterocarpaceae dominate most Asian rainforests, with virtually all of the species in Sri Lanka being endemic to its rainforests. The smaller plants also have high proportions of endemicity, usually indicating forest disturbance levels.
In summary, the lowland rainforests of Sri Lanka, particularly the Sinharaja Forest Reserve, are vital for the conservation of a wide range of endemic flora and fauna, making them an important ecological and biodiversity hotspot in the region.

Dry Zone

content:


The Dry Zone in Sri Lanka refers to a region with a tropical dry broadleaf forest ecoregion, known as the Sri Lanka dry-zone dry evergreen forests. This ecoregion covers most of Sri Lanka and is characterized by flat topography, receiving about 2,000 mm of annual rainfall during the northeast monsoon from December to March, but is mostly dry during the rest of the year. The Dry Zone includes the northern part of the country and much of the east and southeast. The region is significant for agriculture, with varied flow rivers being a major asset for irrigation in the drier parts of the country. The Dry Zone is also a site for the study of aquaculture development within farmer-managed irrigation systems. The dry evergreen forests in this region are made up mostly of evergreen trees, which distinguish them from the deciduous trees that characterize most other tropical dry broadleaf forest ecoregions. The Dry Zone has been the focus of various development programs, including the Mahaweli Development Program, aimed at intensifying crop production systems in the region.

Tropical Lower Montane Forests.

content:


The tropical lower montane forests of Sri Lanka occur above 1,000 m in the Central Highlands and Knuckles mountains, with their best development seen above 1,500 m. These forests are also known as cloud forests due to the condensation of hot air from the lowlands, resulting in afternoon rains. They cover about 1.1% of the land area and are characterized by a marked difference in floristic composition and physiognomy compared to the sub-montane forests of middle elevation. The structure of these forests is less complex, with all trees more or less arranged in a single layer, and their canopies typically reach heights of about 30 feet. Approximately 50% of all species in these forests are endemic to Sri Lanka. The tropical lower montane forests are cooler than lowland forests, providing ideal conditions for the growth of cloud forests. They are rich in biodiversity, with half of Sri Lanka's endemic flowering plants and 51% of the endemic vertebrates being restricted to these forests. These forests are also home to twisted, stunted trees, various orchids, mosses, and ferns. The montane rain forests cover 3,099.5 ha in total, or 0.05% of Sri Lanka's total area, and are found in mountain tops such as Pidurutalagala, Kikilimana, Meepilimana, Agrabopaththalawa, Adam's Peak, and Hakgala. At lower elevations, between 1,000–1,500 m, sub-montane forests occur, accounting for 1.04% of the nation's area.

Wild Life In Sri Lanka

  • Relevant information

    Sri Lanka is a wildlife enthusiast's paradise, offering a diverse range of flora and fauna. The country is home to some of the finest national parks in the subcontinent, providing opportunities to witness a variety of species, including the elusive leopard, regal elephant, blue whale, and a plethora of exotic birds. Here are some of the key highlights of Sri Lankan wildlife

  • Key Wildlife Hotspots

    • Yala National Park: for its population of leopards, Yala National Park is one of the best places in the world for observing and photographing these majestic big cats.
    • Sinharaja Forest Reserve: UNESCO World Heritage Site is a biodiversity hotspot, home to a rich variety of flora and fauna, including many endemic species
    • Udawalawe National Park: for its large population of wild elephants, Udawalawe offers a guaranteed opportunity to see these magnificent creatures in their natural habitat.

  • Unique Wildlife Experiences

    • The Gathering of Elephants: Witness the largest seasonally recurring concentration of wild elephants at Minneriya and Kaudulla National Parks, where over 300 elephants may gather on the seasonally drying lake bed.
    • Blue and Sperm Whales: Lanka offers exceptional opportunities for whale watching, with Mirissa and Trincomalee being among the best locations for observing blue whales, the largest animals on earth, and sperm whales.

  1. Conservation and Biodiversity

    • Sri Lanka boasts a high rate of biological endemism, with approximately 16% of its flora and fauna being unique to the island
    • The country's montane and sub-montane wet forests, such as the Sri Lanka Montane Rainforests, are home to a wealth of endemic species, including flora and fauna that have evolved in isolation over millions of years.
